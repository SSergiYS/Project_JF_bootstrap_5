What is Washoku?

In 2013, at a meeting of the UNESCO Intergovernmental Committee, traditional Japanese cuisine was included in the list of intangible cultural heritage. But it earned worldwide recognition long before that. Its uniqueness, the reason for its high popularity, is based on simple but effective rules:

  • Maximum variety of ingredients and their freshness.
  • The desire not to compete with nature by creating new incredible flavors, but on the contrary, to find a balance of ingredients that would emphasize the natural nuances of each product.
  • Respecting the seasonality of dishes.
  • A healthy and balanced diet according to the principle of ichiju-sansai. It implies that the main meal should include one soup, three kinds of snacks made of protein foods and vegetables, and rice.
  • A close connection with traditional festivals and the strengthening of family ties through eating together.

Dishes that reflect the essence of Japanese cuisine

The cuisine of the Land of the Rising Sun is simple yet remarkably harmonious, exquisite, delicious, and infinitely varied. Its basic principles were formed in the medieval Heian period and are still observed today.

Food in Japan must correspond to the five tastes of gomi: sweet, salty, spicy, sour, and bitter. But there is a sixth, special flavor that the country's cooks know how to reveal masterfully — umami. It is characteristic of many protein products and broths based on them.

The meal must delight all the senses for it to be truly satisfying. That is why the beauty and aesthetics of the design and serving of dishes are highly valued. The principle of seasonality is observed not only in the choice of products for cooking but also in serving. Shape, decor, and colors of tableware are thought over very carefully. It should correspond to the current time of year and harmonize with the ingredients of the served dishes. The five basic colors — white, black, yellow, red, and green — must be on the table in different combinations. And the special table etiquette, which implies the use of hashi sticks, turns any meal into a kind of theatrical performance.

This simple, at first glance, philosophy has raised the gastronomic traditions of Japan to a whole new level. Today, this country's cuisine is the world's heritage and is a source of inspiration for the best cooks on the planet. Getting to know it is one of the best reasons to visit the Land of the Rising Sun.

Sushi

Makizushi (or Maki) is the type of sushi most familiar to foreigners, called rolls outside of Japan. It is served in the form of a cylinder of nori (dry seaweed) cut into 6 or 8 pieces, covering the rice, and stuffing. Sometimes a thin omelet is used as a wrapper.

Varieties sushi
  • Futomaki - differs in its larger size and several types of toppings.
  • Uramaki - maki roll with rice "inside out"
  • Gunkan maki - framed with a strip of seaweed on the outside.
  • Nigiri - pieces of raw fish or shrimp served on a thick bed of rice.
  • Chirashizushi (chirashi sushi/barazushi) - plate with rice and various fillings.
  • Temaki - hand-rolled cone of nori containing rice and other ingredients.
  • Oshizushi - in the form of pressed bars made with a wooden oshibako tool.
  • Inarizushi - bags of deep-fried tofu, thin omelet.
  • Narezushi - is an ancient method of cooking by fermenting fish and rice for months.

Sashimi

Many foreigners who order this dish in Japanese restaurants expect to receive beautifully decorated slices of fish. In fact, the term sashimi refers to any thinly sliced raw food. In addition to fish and seafood, it can be beef (gyusashi), deer meat (shikasashi), chicken (torisashi), and horse meat (basashi). A classic serving of this Japanese food involves eating it with wasabi, ginger, daikon, and perilla (shiso) leaves. A set with shikasashi includes citrus fruits, which bring out the flavor of deer meat more fully. They add green onions when serving basashi.

Premium Ebi Sashimi, Maguro Sashimi © smspsy / Shutterstock

Onigiri

The second name for this popular Japanese breakfast or snack is omusubi. It is rice balls with a variety of fillings, from vegetables to meat and seafood. In Tokyo, for example, onigiri with pickled umeboshi plums, which have a specific sour-salty taste, are popular. They can also be triangular in shape and wrapped in nori. Unlike sushi, which requires special skills and men's hands, girls make onigiri. This dish is inexpensive and so popular that you can buy it literally everywhere, from supermarkets to vending machines.

Onigiri © snapinadil / Shutterstock

Tempura

Another iconic meal is fresh shrimp, fish, and vegetables deep-fried in a tender, crispy batter of egg, ice water, and flour whipped into air bubbles. Once it gets into the boiling oil, it blooms like a flower (hana in Japanese). Therefore, this method of cooking is called hana age. The tempura is accompanied by a special sauce called tentsuyu. This is a broth made from kombu seaweed or dried mackerel, very sweet rice wine mirin, and soy sauce.

Tempura © Mintra Chumpoosueb / Shutterstock

Yakitori

Chicken skewers on bamboo skewers baked on coals are among the most favorite Japanese snacks to go with alcohol. Chefs use all parts of the bird — breast, thigh, skin, liver, heart, and cartilage. Each type of meat is served on a separate skewer. Before cooking, it is marinated in a mixture of rice wine, soy sauce, sugar, and salt and served with tare sauce. In addition to chicken, other types of meat and vegetables may be used. Shiitake mushrooms and enoki mushrooms wrapped in bacon with green onions and shishito peppers are a delicious option.

Yakitori © norikko / Shutterstock

Ramen

In fact, it is a hot soup of fresh wheat noodles in a fragrant broth with various additives. What ingredients one finds in their ramen depends on the region, city, and particular restaurant. There are many variants of this dish and the author's recipes. You should definitely try the noodles with Chashu pork, bamboo shoots Menma, tsukemono pickles, pickled shiitake mushrooms, mung bean sprouts, fresh spinach, green onions, and boiled or fried egg. When it comes to ramen broth, there are four main varieties.

Varieties of broth
  • Shio is transparent and light with a distinctly salty taste.
  • Shoyu is made on the basis of soy sauce; it has a light flavor and a light brown color.
  • Miso is a thick opaque and spicy broth of soybean paste.
  • Tonkotsu is cooked from pork bones for 12 to 20 hours; it has a rich flavor and somewhat dense texture.

Ramen - broth with beef tongue, mushrooms and pickled ajitam egg © Natalia Lisovskaya / Shutterstock

Udon

These are hearty wheat noodles, 2 to 4 mm in diameter, which, unlike ramen, have no eggs in their components. The noodles become soft and elastic when boiled. They eat them both cold and hot. Sometimes udon is used as a side dish to meat and vegetables. But more often, udon is poured with dashi broth, seasoned with rice wine, green onions, and soy sauce. Chicken, shrimp tempura, and tofu are added when serving.

Udon © WildSnap / Shutterstock

Soba

These are what they call gray-brown thin noodles, which are made from buckwheat flour with a small addition of wheat. Soba retains a nice dense consistency when boiled. In summer, it is usually eaten cold and dipped in tsuyu sauce. It is made from dashi broth (kombu seaweed decoction infused with bonito tuna flakes), soy sauce, mirin, and sake. During the cold season, soba is often eaten as noodle soup with hot broth.

Soba with vegetables and beef © Cesarz / Shutterstock

Miso soup

The soup is made from dashi broth and miso paste obtained by fermenting soybeans. Green onions, wakame seaweed, tofu, sweet potatoes, daikon, shrimp, fish, mushrooms, or meat are added. Miso soup is a light and nutritious dish. There are hundreds of regional variations, from the simplest to the most luxurious, which, for example, include crab meat.

Miso soup with salmon © Shaiith / Shutterstock

Tonkatsu

This is a breaded, deep-fried pork cutlet. This simple and hearty dish is often served as a sandwich topping or as part of a complete meal with soup, rice, cabbage salad, and other side dishes. Tonkatsu is topped with the sauce of the same name, which has a dark color, thick consistency, and a sour-sweet taste.

Pork and cabbage tonkatsu with sauce © Brent Hofacker / Shutterstock

Okonomiyaki

A hearty and inexpensive snack that Japanese and visitors alike love is a flatbread fried on a teppan (hot metal stove). The dough consists of grated cabbage, flour, eggs, and a small amount of dashi broth. In the classic version of the preparation, the flatbread is well soaked in a special sauce and sprinkled with dried tuna flakes or chopped aonori seaweed. Any ingredient — pork, seafood, beef, or vegetables — may be chosen as an accompaniment. Cooking techniques and types of toppings vary from city to city.

Okonomiyaki with onion, pickled ginger and sauce © Natasha Breen / Shutterstock

Unagi

This is the name of the Japanese river eel, which is considered a real delicacy because of its delicate and sweet flavor. One of the most popular ways to cook it is in the kabayaki style. The fish is coated in a sweet tare sauce and caramelized over charcoal. If the cooked eel is served on a bed of steamed white rice, the dish is called una don. Fried or smoked unagi can be a component of sushi and miso soup. Unlike other fish, it is never eaten raw.

Unagi kabayaki © NPDstock / Shutterstock

Karaage

This word does not mean the name of the dish, but rather a culinary technique where marinated foods are coated with flour or starch and cooked in a deep fryer. But the Japanese themselves, when ordering karaage, most often mean chicken chopped in small pieces and fried to a nice crisp. This is one of the most popular beer snacks offered in many izakayas. However, fans of seafood and fish should definitely try them as well. The finished karaage is served with a slice of lemon and dipping sauce.

Fried chicken karaage © Hihitetlin / Shutterstock

Wagyu and Kobe beef

One of the most expensive delicacies of Japanese cuisine that you must try is the Wagyu marbled beef steak or its elite Kobe variety. The best way to enjoy this flavorful and melt-in-your-mouth meat is to visit a designated restaurant where it is cooked on a grill or a teppan pan. Chefs use a minimum of spices and salt to avoid ruining the flavor of the beef. It is better to choose a mild or medium cooking degree, so the marbled fat reaches the desired melting point. Seasonal vegetables are served as a side dish.

Wagyu A5 served for Yakiniku (grilled meat) © Artit Wongpradu / Shutterstock

Shabu-shabu

It is not just a dish, but a whole culinary act, where the guest of the restaurant takes an active part. They put a stove in front of them, on which a special kettle with boiling broth is placed. The ingredients are lowered into the pot for a very short time. When boiling, they pop up with a characteristic sound, due to which the dish got its name. It is up to the customer to choose the ingredients for shabu-shabu. Most often, it is thinly sliced beef, but you can also try pork, chicken, duck, crab meat, or lobster. You can also choose tofu, vegetables, and mushrooms, which do not need to be cooked for a long time. They serve two kinds of sauces — goma (based on sesame paste) and ponzu, which includes citrus juice, myrrh, and dashi. They put noodles or rice in the broth left over after cooking and finish the meal with it.

Shabu-shabu © vichie81 / Shutterstock

Takoyaki

One of Japan's most popular snacks comes in the form of balls of tender dough with boiled octopus, pieces of tempura, green onions, and pickled ginger inside. They are fried in cafes and street stalls using a special pan with hemispherical indentations. The finished dish is covered with aromatic takoyaki sauce and Japanese tamago-no-mono mayonnaise and topped with a sprinkling of aonori seaweed and tuna flakes. The appetizer should definitely be eaten hot, as its texture becomes coarser as it cools.

Takoyaki © Sean K / Shutterstock

Gyūdon

It can safely be called one of the favorite lunch options for Tokyo office workers. In Japanese, gyūdon means a bowl of beef. Thin slices of this meat are stewed in an aromatic dashi broth seasoned with onions, myrrh, and soy sauce. They serve it on a bed of steamed or boiled rice with pickled ginger and a spicy dry shichi-mi tōgarashi seasoning. The classic accompaniment is miso soup. It is a simple and hearty meal that quickly gives you energy.

Gyudon made from thinly sliced beef, onions, soy sauce and rice © iMoStudio / Shutterstock

Jiaozi

The product is made of the thinnest dough in the form of a crescent. They can be filled with meat and vegetables seasoned with onions and garlic, seafood, or even fruit. Depending on how they are cooked, they include yaki gyoza (fried in a little oil and water), age gyoza(deep-fried), sui gyoza (boiled), and mushi-gyoza (steamed). Gourmets should definitely try ebi gyoza stuffed with chopped shrimp and rice. The dish is served with a flavorful mixture of soy sauce, rice vinegar, and spicy oil.

Jiaozi © Marina Shanti / Shutterstock

Nattō

One of the most controversial Japanese meals, as even the people themselves are divided into two camps: connoisseurs and haters. Natto is fermented soybeans that are eaten together with rice, green onions, and raw eggs seasoned with soy sauce and karashi mustard. They have a peculiar smell with pronounced notes of ammonia, a sweet and salty taste, and a sticky, gooey consistency that many may find unpleasant. Natto is most often served for breakfast. The beans should be stirred well before eating. The dish is prized not primarily for its taste but also for its medicinal properties, which have been proven in several studies.
